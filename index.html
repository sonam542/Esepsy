The Scienc of Self-Discipline By Peter Hollions

Hi, my name is Sonam Tamang, a third-year Computer Science and Engineering major at The Ohio State University. This website documents my experience applying insights from the book The Science of Self-Discipline by Peter Hollins to improve my focus and build better habits during the Spring 2025 semester. My goal is to share the lessons I learned, how I applied them, and the progress I’ve made so others can benefit too.

Book Overview and Connection to My Goals

I chose The Science of Self-Discipline by Peter Hollins because I wanted to strengthen my ability to stay focused and consistent, especially with challenging tasks like coding assignments and sticking to a gym routine. The book offers actionable strategies to resist temptation, break bad habits, and push forward when motivation fades. These ideas directly supported the personal goals I set for this semester.

My Goals

I focused on two main goals for this Sementer:

1) Stay focused and avoid procrastination on coding assignments. I often get overwhelmed by complex programming tasks and end up procrastinating or avoiding them.

2) Maintain a consistent gym routine by working out at least four times a week. In the past, I struggled with consistency due to distractions or lack of motivation.

I chose these goals because they both require discipline, long-term effort, and the ability to push through discomfort and gain skills I wanted to improve.

Strategies I used

The 10-Minute Rule: To help me stay consistent and overcome procrastination, I’ve been using the 10-Minute Rule — a simple but effective strategy where I commit to starting a task for just 10 minutes. More often than not, once I get started, I find myself working well beyond that initial time. This method has been especially helpful for tackling challenging coding assignments.


To track my progress and build a habit of focused work, I use the Forest app. It visually represents my daily focus sessions, helping me stay motivated and accountable by "planting trees" as I work. Over the past month, I’ve accumulated 12 hours of focused time, which reflects steady effort and growth.

Precommitment Strategy: I removed distractions by using website blockers and placing my phone in another room during deep work sessions. I specifically used StayFocusd (for blocking distracting websites on Chrome) and FocusMe (for blocking both websites and apps) to help maintain focus. These tools significantly reduced the temptation to procrastinate and allowed me to stay in a productive flow state.

Implementation Intentions: To stay consistent with my gym goal, I created specific “if-then” plans that tied my intention to a clear trigger. For example: If it’s Monday at 6 PM, then I go to the gym. This strategy helped turn my goal into an automatic habit by linking it to a time and context I regularly encounter.

Identity-Based habits I started telling myself, “I’m someone who follows through,” and built routines that matched that identity. This mental shift helped me stay consistent.

“The next time you feel that you’re about to give in to an urge or temptation, stop and ask yourself how you will feel ten minutes, ten hours, and ten days from now.”

Tracking My progress

Coding Discipline Tracking: I used a time-tracking app to log how many minutes I spent on deep work each day. Over 3 weeks, I saw an increase in focused study sessions from ~30 minutes/day to over 90 minutes/day, 4–5 times a week.

Major Takeaways and Reflections

Discipline beats motivation. I learned that starting is the hardest part, and if I just push through the initial resistance, everything gets easier.

Small wins create momentum. Whether it was completing a short coding task or finishing a gym session, each win built confidence and kept me moving forward.

Your environment matters. By designing my environment to reduce distractions (no phone, study timer, clear to-do list), I was more productive and focused.

This project changed how I approach my work and habits. I’m no longer waiting to “feel motivated”—I rely on systems, habits, and discipline. I’ve become more mindful about how I use my time and confident in my ability to stick with difficult tasks. While I haven’t perfected everything, I made real progress toward both of my goals.
